# Summary of Different Biases

---

## 1. Cognitive Biases

Systematic patterns of deviation from rationality in judgment and decision-making.

| Bias | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Confirmation bias** | Tendency to search for and favor information that confirms existing beliefs. | A person who believes vaccines are harmful only reads anti-vaccine articles and dismisses scientific consensus. |
| **Anchoring bias** | Over-reliance on the first piece of information encountered when making decisions. | A car salesman shows you a $50,000 car first; a $30,000 car then feels like a bargain. |
| **Availability heuristic** | Overestimating the likelihood of events that come easily to mind. | After seeing news coverage of plane crashes, people fear flying more than driving, despite driving being statistically more dangerous. |
| **Dunning-Kruger effect** | People with limited knowledge overestimate their competence; experts underestimate theirs. | A beginner programmer believes they can build a production app in a weekend after learning "Hello World". |
| **Sunk cost fallacy** | Continuing an endeavor because of past investment rather than future value. | Staying in a failing project because "we've already spent so much time and money on it." |

---

## 2. Machine Learning Biases

Errors introduced into AI/ML models through flawed data, design, or deployment.

| Bias | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Selection/sampling bias** | Training data does not represent the real-world population the model will encounter. | A facial recognition model trained mostly on light-skinned faces performs poorly on darker skin tones. |
| **Label bias** | Annotations in training data reflect the prejudices of human labelers. | A sentiment classifier trained on data labeled by annotators from one culture misreads expressions from another. |
| **Measurement bias** | Systematic error in how features or outcomes are measured across groups. | A healthcare model using "cost of care" as a proxy for health need — wealthier patients show higher need even when sicker patients are underserved. |
| **Aggregation bias** | Applying a single model uniformly across subgroups that have different underlying patterns. | A diabetes prediction model trained on combined male/female data underperforms for women because their symptoms differ. |
| **Feedback loop bias** | Model outputs influence future training data, amplifying existing biases over time. | A recidivism prediction tool flags more people from over-policed neighborhoods, leading to more arrests there, reinforcing the original prediction. |

---

## 3. Statistical Biases

Systematic errors in data collection, study design, or analysis that skew results.

| Bias | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Sampling bias** | The sample used does not accurately reflect the population of interest. | An online survey about internet usage excludes people without internet access, skewing results. |
| **Survivorship bias** | Focusing only on subjects that "survived" a selection process while ignoring those that did not. | Studying successful startups to learn best practices, ignoring the far larger number of failed startups with similar strategies. |
| **Reporting/publication bias** | Studies with positive or significant results are more likely to be published than null results. | A drug appears effective in the literature because trials showing no effect were never published. |
| **Omitted variable bias** | A confounding variable not included in the analysis causes a false relationship between variables. | Ice cream sales and drowning rates are correlated — the omitted variable is hot weather, which drives both. |
| **Observer bias** | The act of measuring or the expectations of the observer influence the outcome. | A researcher unconsciously records ambiguous animal behavior as matching their hypothesis. |

---

## 4. Social/Cultural Biases

Prejudices and stereotypes rooted in social norms that affect how people perceive and treat others.

| Bias | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Implicit/unconscious bias** | Attitudes or stereotypes that affect actions and decisions without conscious awareness. | A hiring manager unconsciously favors resumes with names that sound culturally familiar, even with identical qualifications. |
| **In-group bias** | Favoring members of one's own group over outsiders. | Team members rate their own team's work more favorably than equally good work from another team. |
| **Attribution bias** | Explaining one's own behavior situationally, but attributing others' behavior to their character. | "I was late because of traffic" (situational), but "she's late because she's irresponsible" (dispositional). |
| **Stereotyping** | Applying oversimplified generalizations about a group to every individual in that group. | Assuming an older employee is not tech-savvy simply because of their age. |
| **Halo effect** | A positive impression in one area influences perception in unrelated areas. | Assuming an attractive person is also more intelligent, trustworthy, or competent without evidence. |

---

## Key Takeaway

All four categories of bias share a common thread: they cause systematic, non-random errors in perception, judgment, or data. Recognizing which type of bias is at play is the first step toward mitigating its impact — whether through critical thinking, diverse training data, rigorous study design, or inclusive organizational practices.
