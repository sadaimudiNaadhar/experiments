# Ontology of Indian Philosophy

Indian philosophy (*Bhāratīya Darśana*) is a 3,000-year tradition of inquiry organized historically into schools (*darśanas*) that accept or reject the authority of the Vedas, and thematically around shared problems of metaphysics, epistemology, ethics, and liberation.

---

## 1. Top-Level Taxonomy

```
Indian Philosophy (Darśana)
│
├── Āstika  (Orthodox — accept Vedic authority)
│   ├── Nyāya             (logic, epistemology)
│   ├── Vaiśeṣika         (atomism, categorial ontology)
│   ├── Sāṃkhya           (dualist enumeration of reality)
│   ├── Yoga              (psychological discipline)
│   ├── Pūrva Mīmāṃsā     (ritual hermeneutics, dharma)
│   └── Vedānta (Uttara Mīmāṃsā)
│       ├── Advaita               (Śaṅkara — non-dualism)
│       ├── Viśiṣṭādvaita         (Rāmānuja — qualified non-dualism)
│       ├── Dvaita                (Madhva — dualism)
│       ├── Dvaitādvaita          (Nimbārka — dual-nondual)
│       ├── Śuddhādvaita          (Vallabha — pure non-dualism)
│       └── Acintya Bhedābheda    (Caitanya — inconceivable difference-non-difference)
│
└── Nāstika (Heterodox — reject Vedic authority)
    ├── Buddhism (Bauddha)
    │   ├── Theravāda (Sthaviravāda)
    │   ├── Mahāyāna
    │   │   ├── Madhyamaka      (Nāgārjuna — emptiness)
    │   │   └── Yogācāra        (Asaṅga, Vasubandhu — consciousness-only)
    │   └── Vajrayāna
    ├── Jainism (Jaina)
    │   ├── Digambara
    │   └── Śvetāmbara
    ├── Cārvāka / Lokāyata      (materialism)
    └── Ājīvika                 (radical determinism — extinct)
```

---

## 2. The Six Orthodox Schools (Ṣaḍ-Darśana)

| School | Core Concern | Foundational Text | Founder (trad.) | Key Doctrine |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Nyāya** | Valid knowledge & inference | *Nyāya Sūtras* | Gautama (Akṣapāda) | Four *pramāṇas*; soul as a substance; realism about universals |
| **Vaiśeṣika** | Categories of being | *Vaiśeṣika Sūtras* | Kaṇāda | Atomism (*paramāṇu*); seven *padārthas* |
| **Sāṃkhya** | Cosmology & evolution | *Sāṃkhya Kārikā* | Kapila (Īśvarakṛṣṇa) | Dualism of *Puruṣa* and *Prakṛti*; 25 *tattvas* |
| **Yoga** | Mental discipline | *Yoga Sūtras* | Patañjali | Eightfold path (*aṣṭāṅga*) to *kaivalya* |
| **Pūrva Mīmāṃsā** | Ritual & dharma | *Mīmāṃsā Sūtras* | Jaimini | Vedas as eternal and self-validating; action over knowledge |
| **Vedānta** | Brahman & liberation | *Brahma Sūtras*, *Upaniṣads*, *Bhagavad Gītā* | Bādarāyaṇa | Brahman as ultimate reality; many sub-schools |

The schools are traditionally paired: **Nyāya–Vaiśeṣika** (logic + ontology), **Sāṃkhya–Yoga** (theory + practice), **Mīmāṃsā–Vedānta** (action + knowledge halves of Vedic exegesis).

## 3. The Heterodox Schools

| School | Stance on Self | Stance on Vedas | Goal | Distinctive Doctrine |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Buddhism** | *Anātman* — no permanent self | Reject | *Nirvāṇa* | Four Noble Truths; dependent origination (*pratītyasamutpāda*) |
| **Jainism** | *Jīva* — infinite eternal souls | Reject | *Mokṣa* (liberation of *jīva*) | *Anekāntavāda* (many-sidedness); strict *ahiṃsā* |
| **Cārvāka** | Self = body; perishes at death | Reject | *Sukha* — pleasure in this life | Only *pratyakṣa* (perception) is valid; rejects karma & afterlife |
| **Ājīvika** | Fixed soul, no free will | Reject | None achievable | *Niyati* — absolute determinism |

## 6. Ontological Categories by School

### Vaiśeṣika — Seven *Padārthas*
1. *Dravya* (substance) — earth, water, fire, air, ether, time, space, self, mind
2. *Guṇa* (quality)
3. *Karma* (motion / action)
4. *Sāmānya* (universal)
5. *Viśeṣa* (particular)
6. *Samavāya* (inherence)
7. *Abhāva* (non-existence — added by later commentators)

### Sāṃkhya — Twenty-Five *Tattvas*
- *Puruṣa* — pure consciousness (witness)
- *Prakṛti* — unmanifest primordial nature
- *Mahat / Buddhi* — intellect
- *Ahaṃkāra* — ego-principle
- *Manas* — mind
- 5 *Jñānendriyas* — sense organs
- 5 *Karmendriyas* — organs of action
- 5 *Tanmātras* — subtle elements
- 5 *Mahābhūtas* — gross elements (earth, water, fire, air, ether)

### Jainism — Nine *Tattvas*
*Jīva* (soul), *Ajīva* (non-soul), *Āsrava* (influx of karma), *Bandha* (bondage), *Saṃvara* (stoppage), *Nirjarā* (shedding), *Mokṣa* (liberation), with *Puṇya* (merit) and *Pāpa* (demerit).

### Buddhism — Five *Skandhas*
*Rūpa* (form), *Vedanā* (feeling), *Saṃjñā* (perception), *Saṃskāra* (volitional formations), *Vijñāna* (consciousness).

### Advaita Vedānta — Levels of Reality
- *Pāramārthika* — absolute reality (Brahman)
- *Vyāvahārika* — empirical / transactional reality
- *Prātibhāsika* — illusory / dreamlike appearance

## 7. Foundational Texts

| Period | Tradition | Key Texts |
|---|---|---|
| **Vedic** (~1500–500 BCE) | Vedic / proto-Vedānta | *Ṛgveda*, *Sāmaveda*, *Yajurveda*, *Atharvaveda*; *Brāhmaṇas*, *Āraṇyakas*, *Upaniṣads* |
| **Classical Sūtra** (~500 BCE–500 CE) | Six darśanas | *Brahma Sūtras*, *Yoga Sūtras*, *Nyāya Sūtras*, *Vaiśeṣika Sūtras*, *Sāṃkhya Kārikā*, *Mīmāṃsā Sūtras* |
| **Smṛti / Itihāsa** | Hindu | *Bhagavad Gītā*, *Manusmṛti*, *Mahābhārata*, *Rāmāyaṇa*, *Purāṇas* |
| **Buddhist Canon** | Buddhism | *Tripiṭaka* (Pāli); *Prajñāpāramitā Sūtras*, *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra*, *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā* (Nāgārjuna) |
| **Jain Canon** | Jainism | *Āgamas*; *Tattvārtha Sūtra* (Umāsvāti) |
| **Bhāṣya / Commentary** (~500–1500 CE) | Vedānta & Buddhism | Śaṅkara's *Brahmasūtra Bhāṣya*, Rāmānuja's *Śrī Bhāṣya*, Madhva's *Bhāṣyas*; Buddhaghosa's *Visuddhimagga*; Candrakīrti, Dharmakīrti |
| **Modern** (19th–20th c.) | Neo-Vedānta, syncretic | Vivekānanda, Aurobindo, Radhakrishnan, Krishnamurti, Gandhi, Ambedkar |

## 8. Major Figures and Lineages

**Vedānta**
- *Ādi Śaṅkara* (~8th c.) → *Sureśvara*, *Padmapāda* → Advaita
- *Rāmānuja* (~11th c.) → Viśiṣṭādvaita
- *Madhva* (~13th c.) → Dvaita
- *Caitanya* (~16th c.) → Acintya Bhedābheda

**Buddhism**
- *Gautama Buddha* (~5th c. BCE) → early canon
- *Nāgārjuna* (~2nd c.) → Madhyamaka
- *Asaṅga* and *Vasubandhu* (~4th c.) → Yogācāra
- *Dignāga* and *Dharmakīrti* (~5th–7th c.) → Buddhist epistemology

**Jainism**
- *Pārśvanātha* and *Mahāvīra* (~6th c. BCE) — 23rd and 24th *Tīrthaṅkaras*
- *Umāsvāti* (~2nd c.) — *Tattvārtha Sūtra*
- *Kundakunda* (~2nd c.) — Digambara mysticism

**Logic & Epistemology**
- *Gautama* (Nyāya), *Kaṇāda* (Vaiśeṣika), *Vātsyāyana*, *Udayana*, *Gaṅgeśa* (Navya-Nyāya, ~13th c.)

**Sāṃkhya–Yoga**
- *Kapila*, *Īśvarakṛṣṇa* (*Sāṃkhya Kārikā*), *Patañjali* (*Yoga Sūtras*), *Vyāsa* (commentary)

**Modern**
- *Swami Vivekānanda* (1863–1902) — Neo-Vedānta
- *Sri Aurobindo* (1872–1950) — Integral Yoga
- *S. Radhakrishnan* (1888–1975) — comparative philosophy
- *J. Krishnamurti* (1895–1986) — non-doctrinal inquiry
- *B. R. Ambedkar* (1891–1956) — Navayāna Buddhism

## 9. Principal Polemical Relationships

```
Cārvāka      ⇄  All others       denial of karma, soul, afterlife
Buddhism     ⇄  Vedānta          anātman vs. ātman
Jainism      ⇄  Buddhism         eternal souls vs. no-self
Madhyamaka   ⇄  Yogācāra         emptiness vs. mind-only
Advaita      ⇄  Dvaita           non-dualism vs. dualism
Mīmāṃsā      ⇄  Vedānta          action vs. knowledge as path
Nyāya        ⇄  Buddhism         realism vs. anti-realism about universals
Sāṃkhya      ⇄  Vedānta          two ultimates vs. one Brahman
```

## 10. Cross-Cutting Themes

| Theme | Description |
|---|---|
| **Pramāṇa-vāda** | What counts as valid knowledge — the central inter-school debate |
| **Ātma-vāda vs. Anātma-vāda** | Existence (or not) of a permanent self |
| **Satkārya-vāda vs. Asatkārya-vāda** | Whether the effect pre-exists in the cause (Sāṃkhya/Vedānta) or is newly produced (Nyāya/Buddhism) |
| **Vivartavāda vs. Pariṇāmavāda** | Whether the world is an apparent transformation of Brahman (Advaita) or a real one (Sāṃkhya, other Vedāntins) |
| **Kṣaṇikavāda** | Doctrine of momentariness — everything exists only for an instant (Buddhism) |
| **Anekāntavāda & Syādvāda** | Many-sidedness of reality; conditional predication (Jainism) |
| **Puruṣārtha** | Four legitimate aims of human life: *dharma*, *artha*, *kāma*, *mokṣa* |
| **Vāda traditions** | Formal debate genres: *vāda* (cooperative), *jalpa* (eristic), *vitaṇḍā* (purely destructive) |

---

## Key Takeaway

Indian philosophy is not a single doctrine but a long-running, internally critical conversation organized around shared problems — the self, valid knowledge, action, and liberation — debated across schools that diverge sharply on metaphysics while mostly agreeing on the framing. The *Āstika*/*Nāstika* split is institutional (acceptance of the Vedas); the deeper philosophical alignments cut across it. Sāṃkhya's dualism, Vaiśeṣika's categorial pluralism, Vedāntic monism, Buddhist process-ontology, and Jain quantified pluralism each propose a distinct ontological architecture for the same underlying questions.
